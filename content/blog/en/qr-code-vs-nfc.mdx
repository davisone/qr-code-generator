## Two Technologies, One Goal

QR codes and NFC (Near Field Communication) both allow contactless information transfer. But their modes of operation, costs, and use cases differ significantly.

## How Do They Work?

### QR Code

A visual pattern encoding data, read by a smartphone camera. The phone decodes the pattern and executes the action (open a URL, connect to WiFi, add a contact).

**Range**: from a few centimeters to several meters depending on the QR code size.

### NFC

An electronic chip that communicates via radio frequency with a smartphone held within 4 cm. The phone detects the chip and reads the stored data.

**Range**: 1 to 4 cm maximum.

## Advantages of QR Codes

### Unbeatable Cost
A QR code costs nothing to produce. Printed on paper, displayed on screen, engraved on metal — the medium is the only expense. For a marketing campaign with 10,000 flyers, the difference is massive.

### Visual Range
A QR code on a poster can be scanned from across the street. An NFC tag requires pressing your phone against it. For signage, storefronts, and posters, the QR code wins.

### Universality
100% of smartphones manufactured since 2018 read QR codes natively via the camera. NFC requires a compatible chip (absent on some older or budget models).

### Mass Usage
At an event, 200 people can scan a displayed QR code simultaneously. An NFC tag can only handle one person at a time.

## Advantages of NFC

### Seamless Experience
Tapping your phone is faster than opening the camera and aiming at a QR code. For repeated interactions (access badges, payments), NFC is unbeatable.

### Durability
An encapsulated NFC chip resists water, UV, and wear. A printed QR code degrades over time.

### Discretion
NFC is invisible — perfect for premium products where a visible QR code would be unappealing.

### Works Without Light
NFC works in total darkness. QR codes need light (or the phone's flashlight).

## When to Use What?

### QR Code — ideal for:
- **Marketing**: flyers, posters, packaging, business cards
- **Restaurants**: menus, orders, payments
- **Events**: tickets, schedules, WiFi
- **Education**: course materials, resource links
- **Real estate**: signs, property listings

### NFC — ideal for:
- **Access control**: badges, smart locks
- **Contactless payment**: payment terminals
- **Luxury goods**: authentication, premium experience
- **Logistics**: inventory tracking, traceability
- **Automotive**: digital key sharing

### Both Together
The QR + NFC combination is often the best approach. Examples:
- **Business card**: QR code on the front + embedded NFC chip
- **Trade show booth**: QR code visible from afar + NFC tag for nearby visitors
- **Product**: NFC in the packaging + QR code on the label

<InfoBox type="info">
For the vast majority of marketing and communication use cases, the QR code is the most pragmatic choice: free, universal, customizable, and trackable. NFC excels in repeated physical interactions (access, payment) where speed is the priority.
</InfoBox>
